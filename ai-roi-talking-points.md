# AI ROI — Executive Talking Points
**Audience:** SVP+ · Enterprise AI Programme  
**Purpose:** Communicate AI dollar value and scalability without triggering budget reduction  
**Format:** Talking points — speak to these, do not read them

---

## Opening Frame

> "We're fully aligned on demonstrating AI's dollar value at scale — and I want to make sure we're measuring it in a way that actually captures the real return when this is replicated across the enterprise."

Sets the tone: you're on side with the cost mandate, and you have something more credible than a simple time-saved number.

---

## Talking Point 1 — We built a structured, repeatable model. Not ad hoc AI use.

Most teams using AI today are doing it individually and inconsistently. We built something different — a compound engineering workflow where every role has a defined agent, a defined artifact, and a defined handoff. That structure is what makes measurement possible, and what makes it replicable.

**Why this matters for scale:** You can't scale ad hoc. You can scale a model. What we've built in Treasury Technology is designed to be picked up by any delivery team in the enterprise — same agents, same workflow, same measurement framework. The investment we've made in the model is an enterprise asset, not a team-specific experiment.

---

## Talking Point 2 — Time saved per task is the wrong unit. Capacity at scale is the right one.

If we measure AI as "X% time saved per developer" and report that to the enterprise, two things happen. Teams are incentivised to inflate their numbers to look good. And leadership interprets it as a budget reduction opportunity — the same work, cheaper. Both outcomes undermine the programme.

The right unit is: **how many more high-value delivery cycles can the enterprise run with the same headcount?** A team that was delivering 40 features a quarter and can now deliver 48 — with better quality and less rework — has created enterprise value that compounds as you scale it across 10, 20, 50 teams.

> "The enterprise dollar case for AI isn't doing the same work cheaper. It's doing materially more work at the same cost — and measuring that at portfolio level, not task level."

---

## Talking Point 3 — The savings need to be reinvested, not extracted. Especially during the scaling phase.

We're early in this. The agents we have today are good. The agents we'll have in 12 months will be significantly more capable — but only if we have the engineering capacity to build, maintain, and improve them. Every team that adopts this model will need that same investment: building their own agents, training their champions, tuning prompts, governing the reference library.

If the enterprise response to early productivity gains is to cut budgets, it kills the programme at exactly the moment it needs investment to scale. The teams that haven't adopted yet will look at what happened to the early adopters and make a rational decision not to bother.

**The right enterprise play:** reinvest the first wave of gains into scaling the model faster. The compounding effect of 20 teams running this workflow is worth far more than the cost savings from trimming 3 teams that got there first.

---

## Talking Point 4 — Treasury Technology is the proof of concept. Here's what replication looks like.

We're not asking the enterprise to take this on faith. We have a working model, active agents, real ticket data, and a measurement framework in place. What we can offer is a replication playbook — the agents, the prompt library, the workflow, the governance structure — that any delivery team can adopt without starting from scratch.

**The enterprise dollar case:** instead of every team spending 6–12 months figuring this out independently, they get a proven model on day one. The productivity gains arrive faster, the measurement framework is consistent across teams, and leadership gets comparable data across the portfolio instead of 20 different teams measuring different things differently.

Quantify it for them: if it took us **[X months]** to get to a working compound chain, and we can compress that to **[Y months]** for the next team using our playbook, that's **[Z months]** of productive AI use brought forward per team. At enterprise scale, that's material.

---

## Talking Point 5 — Here's the measurement commitment. Consistent across every team that adopts this.

For this to work at enterprise scale, every team needs to measure the same things the same way. We've already built that framework:

- Every Jira ticket is tagged when AI is used — mandatory field on ticket close
- We track cycle time, throughput, and defect rate — AI-assisted vs non-assisted tickets, controlled for complexity
- We report quarterly: what AI freed up, and what we deployed that capacity against

If the enterprise adopts this framework, leadership gets a consistent, comparable view across all teams. Not 20 different productivity claims that can't be aggregated — **one methodology that produces one portfolio-level number** that leadership can actually use.

**The ask:** standardise this measurement framework across the enterprise from the start. Don't let teams self-report in isolation. That's how you get numbers that are credible enough to put in front of the board.

---

## Closing Line

Use this to close the conversation:

> "The enterprise question isn't whether AI saves time. It's whether we're building a programme that compounds. Done right, every team that joins makes the model better for every team that follows. Done wrong — measured as cost reduction and budgeted accordingly — we slow it down at exactly the wrong moment."

---

## Key Rules for This Conversation

| Never say | Say instead |
|-----------|-------------|
| "AI saves us X% of our time" | "AI creates X% more delivery capacity per sprint" |
| "We can do the same work with fewer people" | "We can absorb more work without adding headcount" |
| "We're saving money" | "We're reinvesting capacity into higher-value work" |
| "This reduces cost" | "This compounds value as we scale it" |
